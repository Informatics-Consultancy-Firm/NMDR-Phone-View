NMCP Suspected Malaria (Fever) Cases Extract

Purpose. This tool extracts monthly suspected malaria (fever) cases from DHIS2 for every health facility in Sierra Leone — from the old facility form, the new HF01 facility form and community reporting — cleans them, attaches Public/Private ownership, and produces analysis-ready tables. Every step below is applied automatically on each run.
1
Fetch the facility registry
All Level-5 (PHU) org units are pulled from DHIS2 with their full hierarchy — District (Level 2) and Chiefdom (Level 4).

Level-6 units (CHW / ETR posts) are also pulled. Community suspected cases are reported at Level 6, not at the facility, so each Level-6 unit is mapped to its Level-5 PHU parent and its values are rolled up to that facility. Level-6 units never appear as facilities in their own right — they only contribute community cases to their parent PHU.
2
Apply exclusions before any data is fetched
A facility is dropped entirely — appearing in no analysis, denominator, or export — if it is:
  • A laboratory (name contains "Laboratory"). These are not health facilities, yet many are typed Hospital in the source (e.g. "Bo Regional Hospital Laboratory"), which would inflate the Hospital count.
  • A Marie Stopes facility — excluded by NMCP request (specialised SRH provider).
  • Of an unrecognised type — the type cannot be derived from the facility name.
Facility types are normalised to five categories: Hospital, Clinic, CHC, CHP, MCHP. Misspellings are folded in ("Climic" → Clinic), as are nursing homes ("Home" → Clinic).

The full list of excluded facilities, with the reason for each, is shown and downloadable after the run.
3

Monthly values are pulled from the DHIS2 analytics API for every month in the selected range.
Three sources. Suspected malaria (fever) cases come from an old facility form, a new facility form (HF01 rollout), and a community data element. All three are fetched and mapped onto three common age groups: 0-59m, 05-14y, 15+y.

The community under-5 band is 2-59 months (CHWs do not treat neonates). It is treated as the 0-59m group so that facility and community align on the same three age groups.
Level-6 rollup. Community cases are reported by CHW / ETR posts at Level 6, which are children of the PHU. Analytics is therefore queried at LEVEL-5 and LEVEL-6, and every Level-6 value is rolled up to its Level-5 PHU parent. A facility's Community figure is the sum of all its CHW/ETR posts. Facility forms are never reported at Level 6, so any Level-6 value for a facility data element is ignored.
The computation
Facility <age> = OLD FACILITY FORM + NEW FACILITY FORM  (each within its own cutover window)

Community <age> = wfaQBIVQxWo  (all periods)

Overall <age> = Facility <age> + Community <age>
Total Fever Suspected Cases = Overall 0-59m + Overall 05-14y + Overall 15+y
This gives 6 series — Facility × 3 age groups and Community × 3 age groups. Outlier detection runs on each series independently, so facility and community cases are never mixed before cleaning.

4
Attach ownership (Public / Private)
Each facility is matched to the NMCP PHU ownership master list, in this order:
  1. DHIS2 OU ID — exact, and immune to renames or spelling changes
  2. Chiefdom (Level 4) + Facility name (Level 5)
  3. Facility name alone
Example — why the OU ID must come first
Two different facilities in DHIS2 share the exact same name:
OU IDDistrictChiefdom (L4)Facility (L5)

Matching on name alone cannot tell these apart. Matching on the OU ID resolves each one exactly — and keeps working even if NMCP later renames a facility or fixes a typo in DHIS2.
5
Detect and correct outliers IQR method
Applied independently to each facility × each year × each age group. Only reported values (non-missing, greater than zero) are used.
Q1 = 25th percentile  ·  Q3 = 75th percentile  ·  IQR = Q3 − Q1
Upper bound = Q3 + 3.0 × IQR   →   anything above it is replaced by the median
Worked example — one facility, 0-59m, 12 months of one year
Step A · The 12 monthly values, sorted
110, 115, 120, 125, 128, 130, 132, 135, 140, 145, 150, 900
Step B · Find the quartiles (they split the sorted data)
Q1 (25th pct)123.75a quarter of months fall below this
Median (50th pct)131the facility's typical month
Q3 (75th pct)141.25three quarters fall below this
Step C · Measure the normal spread
IQR = Q3 − Q1 = 141.25 − 123.75 = 17.5
The middle 50% of this facility's months span just 17.5 cases — it is a steady facility.
Step D · Set the fence
Upper bound = Q3 + 3.0 × IQR = 141.25 + 3.0 × 17.5 = 193.75
Step E · Flag and replace
900 > 193.75  →  Outlier = Yes  →  replaced by the median  →  131
All other 11 months sit below the fence and are left completely untouched.
The same thing, drawn
What is NOT touched
  • Zeros stay zero. A facility that genuinely saw 0 patients keeps its 0 — it is never flagged, and never used to compute the bound or the median.
  • Missing stays missing. A month with no report stays blank; the tool never invents a value for it.
  • Fewer than 4 reported values? No bound is computed (quartiles would be unstable) and nothing is corrected.
  • Values below the bound are never altered — even the highest one. In the example, 150 is the biggest normal month and survives untouched.
Why 3.0 and not 1.5? Tukey calls 1.5×IQR an "outlier" fence and 3.0×IQR a "far outlier" fence. Because a flagged value is replaced by the median — a big change, not a gentle trim — a false alarm is costly. Malaria is seasonal, so a rainy-season month is legitimately two to three times a dry-season month. The wide 3.0 fence lets those real peaks through and reserves correction for values that are almost certainly data-entry errors (a stray digit, a duplicated entry).

Why the median, not the bound? The median is robust — that 900 barely moves it (131 with the 900, 130 without). So the replacement is not contaminated by the very value it is replacing, and the facility ends up with a plausible typical month rather than the extreme threshold that happened to catch it.
6
Classify facilities as Active or Inactive
  • Active — reported at least one suspected-case value during the selected period.
  • Inactive — present in the DHIS2 registry but reported no suspected cases at all for the period.
Example — why inactive facilities are removed from the denominator
Suppose a group has 4 private facilities. Facility D never reports at all in the period:
FacilityReported at least once?Status
AYesActive
BYesActive
CYesActive
DNo — never, all periodInactive
In a month where all 3 active facilities reported:
If all 4 were counted:   3 ÷ 4 = 75% — this reads as a reporting failure
Active facilities only (the method used here):   3 ÷ 3 = 100% — every functioning facility did report
Facility D is not a reporting problem; it is an existence problem. Separating the two keeps both measurable: activity is reported in its own analysis, and the reporting rate stays a clean measure of reporting behaviour.
7
Build a complete period grid
For every active facility, a row is created for every month of the selected range — including months with no data. Those appear explicitly with Reported = No rather than silently disappearing, so reporting gaps are visible and countable.
Example — a facility that did not report in June
MonthTotal Fever Suspected CasesReported
Apr103Yes
May104Yes
Jun(blank)No
Jul106Yes
June is kept as a row with no value and Reported = No. The facility therefore contributes 12 rows for the year, not 11.
This is what makes reporting gaps countable: a month that is absent from the data cannot be measured. Keeping it visible means June correctly lowers that month's reporting rate.
8
Calculate reporting rates
Reporting rate = (facilities reporting suspected cases that month ÷ ACTIVE facilities in that group) × 100
Computed per month, broken down by ownership (Public vs Private) and by facility type (CHC, CHP, MCHP, Hospital, Clinic).
Example — March, Public facilities
Active public facilities in the period: 1,200
Public facilities that reported suspected cases in March: 1,044

Reporting rate = 1,044 ÷ 1,200 × 100 = 87.0%
Read as: of the public facilities that actually function, 87% submitted suspected-case data in March. The other 13% are genuine reporting gaps worth chasing.
9
Produce the outputs
Each age group carries four columns, in this order:
  • Original — the raw value from DHIS2, before any correction
  • Upper Bound — the threshold it was tested against (Q3 + 3.0 × IQR)
  • Outlier — Yes if the original exceeded the bound
  • Corrected — equals Original when clean; equals the facility-year median when replaced
Plus Total Fever Suspected Cases and Reported.

Reading the results. Because outliers are replaced by the median rather than merely trimmed, corrected totals sit slightly below raw totals wherever a correction occurred. Check the per-variable outlier rate after each run: a high rate (say above ~5% of data points) usually means genuine seasonality is being flagged, not errors — worth inspecting before the figures are used.
